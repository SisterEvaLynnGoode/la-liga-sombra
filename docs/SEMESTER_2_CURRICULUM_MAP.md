## 1. Premise — "La Liga Sombra a través del Tiempo"

A new antagonist — a **time thief** (working name **El Cronista** / *"La Sombra del
Tiempo"*) — is stealing each culture's signature treasure **from its own historical era**,
before it can become that country's national patrimony. The agent uses La Liga Sombra's
recovered time-machine to jump into each moment, gather clues, and recover the artifact.

This keeps the **exact stolen-treasure formula every Semester 1 caso already uses** — only
now the crime scene is a moment in history instead of a modern city.

### Why time-travel + "go slow" are compatible: the historical present

Standing in the past does **not** force past-tense grammar. Spanish naturally uses the
**historical present** to narrate past events vividly ("El ladrón *entra*, *toma* el mapa,
*sale* corriendo…"). So students narrate 1914 Panamá or 700-AD Copán **in the present tense
they are still consolidating.** The setting delivers culture, novelty, and ACTFL cultural
competency; the grammar stays in review mode until the last two units crack the past-tense
door.

**Consolidation load:** Units 11–18 are all review / Spanish-1-level. Only Units 19–20
open the past tense, and only at the recognition + set-phrase level (no full preterite
conjugation drills). This matches the "go slower" decision.

### Cultural-sensitivity filter
All eras chosen are **celebratory** — archaeology, sports, poetry, engineering, music,
craft. We deliberately avoid colonization violence, dictatorships, and wars. Keep this
filter when fleshing out each caso's dialogue and reading passages.

## 5. Candidate new game mechanics (mobile + Chromebook friendly)

**Hard constraints for anything new:**
- Touch-first; must work with trackpad too. Tap targets ≥ 44 px.
- **No reliable mic or camera** (Chromebook mics are flaky and often blocked by district
  policy). Prefer tap/drag over voice.
- Low CPU/GPU — no WebGL physics or heavy animation. Target 1366×768 Chromebooks.
- Portrait-friendly and low-bandwidth; degrade gracefully offline.
- Keyboard-**optional** (many students on phones have no keyboard).

Proposed additions, each tied to the grammar it reinforces:

1. **Swipe-to-sort ("Clasifica")** — a sentence/card appears; swipe left/right (or tap two
   big buttons) to classify it. *The* mechanic for **ser vs. estar** (U12) and later
   **present vs. preterite recognition** (U19–20). One-handed, phone-native, near-zero CPU.
2. **Máquina del Tiempo dial** — a rotary date-setter; students dial the correct
   **day / month / year in Spanish** to "jump" to the right era. Reinforces numbers, months,
   and dates (recycles S1). On-theme with time-travel; tap +/- or drag.
3. **Tap-bank cloze ("Rellena")** — a sentence with a blank; tap the correct word from a
   small bank. **Keyboard-free** workhorse for gustar/object pronouns/agreement. Lighter and
   more trackpad-friendly than the existing drag-based sentenceBuilder.
4. **Historical-present scene ("Narra la escena")** — a single static illustrated crime
   scene with tappable hotspots; tap a character/object, then pick the present-tense verb
   describing what they're doing. Teaches present-tense narration inside a past setting —
   the pedagogical heart of the whole semester. Static image + taps = Chromebook-safe.
5. **Routine timeline ("Ordena el día")** — tap-to-place the daily-routine steps
   (se despierta → se ducha → se viste) in order along a timeline. For reflexives (U16).

**Explicitly avoided:** anything mic/camera-dependent (Chromebook reliability), WebGL/physics
mini-games (CPU), and drag-heavy interactions as the *only* input path (hard on trackpads —
always offer a tap fallback).

**Recommended first build:** the **swipe-to-sort** mechanic, debuting in Unit 12 (ser vs.
estar). It's the highest pedagogical payoff, the most mobile-native, and the cheapest to
render — the best proof-of-concept for the new-mechanic pattern.

call for them.
